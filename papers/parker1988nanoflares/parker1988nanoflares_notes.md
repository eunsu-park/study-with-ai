# 21. Nanoflares and the Solar X-Ray Corona / лӮҳл…ён”Ңл Ҳм–ҙмҷҖ нғңм–‘ Xм„  мҪ”лЎңлӮҳ

---

## 1. Core Contribution / н•өмӢ¬ кё°м—¬

### English
Parker proposes that the active X-ray corona is heated not by waves but by **a swarm of nanoflares вҖ” magnetic reconnection events of $\sim 10^{24}$ erg, $10^9$ times smaller than ordinary flares**. The hypothesis rests on three pillars: (1) **observational** вҖ” high-time-resolution X-ray and EUV data from Lin et al. (1984), Brueckner & Bartoe (1983), and Porter et al. (1984, 1987) reveal the corona as a sea of impulsive bursts ranging downward in energy from $10^{27}$ erg to the instrumental cutoff at $10^{24}$ erg. (2) **Theoretical** вҖ” Parker's own theorem that an arbitrarily braided coronal magnetic field cannot relax to a smooth equilibrium but must form **tangential discontinuities (current sheets)** that release magnetic energy via rapid reconnection. (3) **Energetic** вҖ” a remarkably simple Poynting-flux argument: the rate at which the photospheric convection does work on the coronal field is $W \approx (B^2/4\pi)(v^2 t/L)$. With observed values ($B = 100$ G, $v = 0.5$ km sвҒ»В№, $L = 10^{10}$ cm) this reaches the coronal heating requirement of $\sim 10^7$ erg cmвҒ»ВІ sвҒ»В№ in $t = 5 \times 10^4$ s, when the field-line inclination $\theta$ reaches $\approx 14В°$ and reconnection turns on. The estimated energy stored in the **characteristic** discontinuity is $\mathcal{E} = l^2 \Delta L B_\perp^2 / 8\pi \approx 6 \times 10^{24}$ erg, matching the observed nanoflare scale. The paper coined the term *nanoflare*, which has since defined a 35-year research program in coronal heating.

## 2. Reading Notes / мқҪкё° л…ёнҠё

### Part I: Introduction вҖ” The Coronal Heating Problem (p. 474) / мҪ”лЎңлӮҳ к°Җм—ҙ л¬ём ң

#### English
The paper opens by establishing the basic facts:
- Active X-ray corona: $T = 2$вҖ“$3 \times 10^6$ K, embedded in $\sim 100$ G bipolar fields.
- Heat input requirement: $\sim 10^7$ erg cmвҒ»ВІ sвҒ»В№ (Withbroe & Noyes 1977).
- Surface brightness is **essentially independent of bipole size** вҖ” from $10^{10}$ cm normal active regions down to $10^9$ cm X-ray bright points and $2 \times 10^8$ cm small bipoles. This scale-invariance is itself a strong clue.
- Once heat is supplied, the rest is hydrostatic: gas at density $\sim 10^{10}$ cmвҒ»Ві, pressure 6вҖ“9 dyn cmвҒ»ВІ, sound speed $2$вҖ“$3 \times 10^7$ cm sвҒ»В№, AlfvГ©n speed $2 \times 10^8$ cm sвҒ»В№, plasma $\beta \ll 1$ вҶ’ field is force-free.

Two heating-mechanism families are reviewed:
1. **Wave heating** вҖ” Acoustic and gravity waves are dissipated/refracted before reaching the corona; only AlfvГ©n waves penetrate. But AlfvГ©n waves are **disinclined to dissipate**, exactly the property that lets them reach the corona. Various dissipation tricks (resonance absorption, phase mixing) have been proposed (Hayvaerts & Priest 1983, Hollweg 1984, Davila 1987). Required wave amplitude must be < 25 km/s (observational upper limit, Cheng et al. 1979), so dissipation must occur within ~one period вҖ” implying turbulent eddies.
2. **DC/reconnection heating** вҖ” Photospheric convection shuffles field-line footpoints. Even a *continuous* mapping of footpoints generates **tangential discontinuities** (Syrovatsky 1971, Parker 1972, 1979вҖ“1986, Moffatt 1985) that dissipate by reconnection.

Parker explicitly favors the second view but acknowledges observations of footpoint motions are not yet good enough to settle the matter (magnetic fibrils 200 km/0.2вҖі are barely resolved).

### Part II: Observations вҖ” The Sea of Impulsive Bursts (pp. 475вҖ“476) / кҙҖмёЎ: м¶©кІ© нҸӯл°ңмқҳ л°”лӢӨ

#### English
The observational case is built from four sources, each providing a different energy/timescale window:

**(1) Lin et al. (1984) вҖ” Hard X-ray spikes**
- Detector at Earth orbit, > 20 keV.
- Individual spike duration: 1вҖ“2 s.
- Energy range: $10^{24}$ erg (instrumental cutoff) up to $10^{27}$ erg.
- Total Sun output in spikes: $\sim 10^{27}$ erg/s = $2 \times 10^4$ erg cmвҒ»ВІ sвҒ»В№.
- Clusters of spikes every ~300 s constitute "microflares" (5вҖ“100 s duration).
- *Most emitted energy is in the smaller spikes* вҖ” the spectrum is bottom-heavy.

**(2) Brueckner & Bartoe (1983) вҖ” EUV "turbulent events"**
- Localized churning at 100 km/s.
- Mass per event: $10^{10}$ g; KE ~ $7 \times 10^{23}$ erg; lifetime ~ 100 s.
- Rate: $10^3$ new events per second over the entire Sun вҶ’ $10^5$ events in progress at any time.
- Total: $6 \times 10^{26}$ erg/s = $10^4$ erg cmвҒ»ВІ sвҒ»В№.

**(3) Brueckner et al. (1986) вҖ” EUV pinpoint jets**
- Velocity: 400 km/s; mass: $4 \times 10^{11}$ g; KE: $3 \times 10^{26}$ erg.
- Rate: 20 per second; lifetime: 50 s вҶ’ $10^3$ jets at any time.
- Total: $6 \times 10^{27}$ erg/s = $10^5$ erg cmвҒ»ВІ sвҒ»В№.

**(4) Porter, Toomre & Gebbie (1984); Porter et al. (1987) вҖ” UV bright points**
- C IV / Si IV / O IV emission line brightenings in $2$вҖ“$4 \times 10^8$ cm bipoles.
- Lifetimes ~ 100 s; brightness fluctuations 20вҖ“100% on 20вҖ“60 s timescales.
- Larger bipoles вҶ’ more frequent nanoflares вҶ’ continuous brightening (10% are "always on").

**Parker's nanoflare estimate from Porter et al. (1984)**: A region of $4 \times 10^{16}$ cmВІ emitting at $10^7$ erg cmвҒ»ВІ sвҒ»В№ produces $4 \times 10^{23}$ erg/s, or $8 \times 10^{24}$ erg in 20 s. If a single nanoflare contributes 20% of this:
$$\boxed{\mathcal{E}_\text{nano} \approx 1.6 \times 10^{24} \text{ erg}}$$

With ~5 nanoflares simultaneously active, fluctuations of $\sim 5^{-1/2} \approx 44\%$ are predicted вҖ” consistent with the observed 20вҖ“100%.

The terminology is fixed: a **nanoflare** is *any* localized impulsive energy release below the conventional microflare ($\geq 10^{27}$ erg) threshold. **Microflares are themselves clusters of nanoflares** (Parker 1987b; Sturrock et al. 1984).

### Part III: Implications вҖ” Reconnection, Not Waves (p. 476) / н•Ёмқҳ: нҢҢлҸҷмқҙ м•„лӢҢ мһ¬кІ°н•©

#### English
The wave-heating paradigm runs into a critical-thinking problem: to heat a small bipole of $2$вҖ“$10 \times 10^8$ cm, an AlfvГ©n wave must have wavelength $\lesssim$ bipole size, requiring period $1$вҖ“$10$ s. But:
- Such short waves can't be a Kolmogorov cascade from granule motions (300 s) or oscillations (100 s).
- Kolmogorov: $v(l) \propto l^{1/3}$, $\tau = l/v \propto l^{2/3}$, kinetic energy density $\rho v^2 \propto \tau$.
- So 2вҖ“10 s waves have **10вҖ“50Г— less energy** than 100 s waves вҖ” totally inadequate.

Therefore, if the small bipoles really emit at $10^7$ erg cmвҒ»ВІ sвҒ»В№, wave heating fails. The reconnection alternative is:
- Random shuffling of footpoints вҶ’ tangential discontinuities accumulate.
- Each grows until $|\Delta B|$ exceeds an instability threshold вҶ’ explosive reconnection burst (a nanoflare).
- In steady state, creation and destruction balance.
- "We expect the bipole fields above the surface of the Sun to be filled with small-scale reconnection events, i.e., filled with nanoflares."

This naturally explains the observed scale-invariance вҖ” nanoflares occur at a rate per unit area roughly independent of bipole size.

### Part IV: Discussion вҖ” Quantitative Setup (pp. 476вҖ“477) / л…јмқҳ: м •лҹүм Ғ м„Өм •

#### English
Parker confronts the unresolved fibril motions: assumed values are $v \sim 0.5$ km/s (matching granule motions of 1вҖ“2 km/s) with correlation length $l$ ~ granule radius (~250 km).

Theoretical caveat: a quantitative reconnection rate at a specified discontinuity is not available because the dynamics are nonlinear and complex. But based on lab plasma experience:
- Discontinuity strength $|\Delta B|$ grows from zero with slow reconnection.
- When $|\Delta B|$ exceeds threshold вҶ’ runaway dynamical instability вҶ’ explosive reconnection ("hydromagnetic and plasma turbulence") that further accelerates the reconnection.
- Analogous to laboratory **disruptive instabilities** in fusion plasmas (Rosenbluth et al. 1973, Kadomtsev 1975).

Hydromagnetic waves may play a triggering role (Sakai, Tajima, Brunel, Matthaeus & Lamkin 1985вҖ“1986) вҖ” explosion at one site triggers neighbors. But the observed background wave amplitude $\langle v^2 \rangle^{1/2}/V_A \approx 10^{-2}$ is small, so this triggering is uncertain.

### Part V: Inferences вҖ” The Energy Argument (pp. 477вҖ“478) / м¶”лЎ : м—җл„Ҳм§Җ л…јмҰқ

#### English
The crown jewel of the paper. Parker derives a clean order-of-magnitude argument for the heating flux.

**Setup**: Initial uniform vertical field $B$ extending from $z=0$ (photosphere) to $z=L$ (top of coronal loop). Lower footpoint moves with speed $v$, upper footpoint fixed.

**Step 1 вҖ” Tilt angle (Eq. 1)**:
$$\tan\theta(t) \approx \frac{vt}{L}, \quad \theta \ll 1$$

**Step 2 вҖ” Horizontal field (Eq. 2)**:
$$B_\perp = B \tan\theta \approx \frac{Bvt}{L}$$

**Step 3 вҖ” Power input via Maxwell stress (Eq. 3)**:
The footpoint, moving against the magnetic tension $B_\perp B / (4\pi)$, does work at rate:
$$\boxed{W \approx \frac{v B_\perp B}{4\pi} = \frac{B^2}{4\pi}\,\frac{v^2 t}{L} \quad [\text{erg cm}^{-2}\,\text{s}^{-1}]}$$

The power input **grows linearly with time** вҖ” magnetic free energy accumulates.

**Step 4 вҖ” Saturation**: Setting $W = 10^7$ erg cmвҒ»ВІ sвҒ»В№ with $B = 100$ G, $v = 0.5$ km/s, $L = 10^{10}$ cm gives:
$$t = 5 \times 10^4 \text{ s}, \quad B_\perp \approx \frac{B}{4} = 25 \text{ G}, \quad \theta \approx 14В°$$

At this $\theta \approx 14В°$, reconnection ignites and destroys $B_\perp$ as fast as motion produces it. **Steady state**.

**Counter-intuitive corollary**: If reconnection were *less* efficient, $B_\perp$ would grow further, $W$ would increase. **Heat input is inversely proportional to dissipation efficiency.**

**Step 5 вҖ” Discontinuity strength**: $|\Delta B| \sim B_\perp \approx 25$ G.

**Step 6 вҖ” Random walk geometry**: In time $t = 5 \times 10^4$ s at $v = 0.5$ km/s, footpoint traverses pathlength $vt = 2.5 \times 10^4$ km = supergranule diameter (a beautiful coincidence!). With granule lifetime $\tau = 500$ s: each random step has length $l = v\tau = 250$ km, total $n = t/\tau = 100$ random steps.

**Step 7 вҖ” Wrapping geometry**: Each flux bundle undergoes 100 random wraps along its length $L$. Each wrap occupies $\Delta L \approx L/n = 10^3$ km along the bundle.

**Step 8 вҖ” Characteristic nanoflare energy**:
$$\boxed{\mathcal{E} = \frac{l^2 \Delta L\, B_\perp^2}{8\pi} \approx 6 \times 10^{24} \text{ erg}}$$

Plugging in $l = 250$ km, $\Delta L = 10^3$ km, $B_\perp = 25$ G. This is the **free energy stored in one wrap**, which is a *bound* on the nanoflare energy. Most nanoflares should be smaller (incomplete release), and their average energy ~ $10^{24}$ erg matches Lin et al.'s instrumental cutoff and Parker's earlier estimate.

**Step 9 вҖ” Scale-invariance**: For X-ray bright points with $L = 10^4$ km (10Г— smaller than active-region loops), the saturation time becomes $t = 5 \times 10^3$ s (1.4 hr) вҖ” only 10 random steps вҖ” but $l$, $\Delta L$, and $\mathcal{E}$ are unchanged. **Nanoflare characteristics are independent of the loop scale $L$**, explaining the observed scale-invariance of X-ray brightness.

### Part VI: Concluding Notes / л§Ҳл¬ҙлҰ¬ л…ёнҠё

#### English

- Most common nanoflares: ~$10^{24}$ erg (close to Lin et al. instrumental cutoff). Improved instruments may reveal smaller average energies.

## 3. Key Takeaways / н•өмӢ¬ мӢңмӮ¬м җ

1. **The "nanoflare" is born / "лӮҳл…ён”Ңл Ҳм–ҙ"мқҳ нғ„мғқ** вҖ” Parker coined this term to refer to *any* impulsive coronal energy release below the microflare threshold ($\geq 10^{27}$ erg). Specifically, $\sim 10^{24}$ erg events. This terminology now defines a major branch of solar physics. / Parkerк°Җ л§ҲмқҙнҒ¬лЎңн”Ңл Ҳм–ҙ н•ңкі„($\geq 10^{27}$ erg) лҜёл§Ңмқҳ *лӘЁл“ * м¶©кІ©м Ғ мҪ”лЎңлӮҳ м—җл„Ҳм§Җ л°©м¶ңмқ„ к°ҖлҰ¬нӮӨлҠ” мқҙ мҡ©м–ҙлҘј л§Ңл“Ұ. кө¬мІҙм ҒмңјлЎң $\sim 10^{24}$ erg мӮ¬кұҙ. мқҙ мҡ©м–ҙк°Җ нҳ„мһ¬ нғңм–‘л¬јлҰ¬н•ҷмқҳ мЈјмҡ” л¶„м•јлҘј м •мқҳ.

2. **Wave heating is in trouble for small bipoles / нҢҢлҸҷ к°Җм—ҙмқҖ мһ‘мқҖ м–‘к·№м—җм„ң кіӨкІҪ** вҖ” To heat $2$вҖ“$10 \times 10^8$ cm bipoles, AlfvГ©n waves need 1вҖ“10 s periods. Kolmogorov scaling shows such waves carry 10вҖ“50Г— less energy than 100-s waves. ## 4. Mathematical Summary / мҲҳн•ҷм Ғ мҡ”м•Ҫ

### Geometric setup / кё°н•ҳ м„Өм •
Vertical coronal loop of length $L$, photospheric footpoint at $z=0$ moves with speed $v$, top footpoint at $z=L$ fixed.

### Tilt angle / кё°мҡёкё° к°Ғ (Eq. 1)
$$\tan\theta(t) \approx \frac{vt}{L}, \quad \theta \ll 1$$

For $t = 5 \times 10^4$ s, $v = 0.5$ km/s, $L = 10^{10}$ cm: $\tan\theta = 0.25$, $\theta \approx 14В°$.

### Horizontal field component / мҲҳнҸү мһҗкё°мһҘ м„ұл¶„ (Eq. 2)
$$B_\perp = B \tan\theta \approx \frac{Bvt}{L}$$

For $B = 100$ G, $t = 5 \times 10^4$ s: $B_\perp \approx 25$ G.

### Power input from photospheric work / кҙ‘кө¬ мқјм—җм„ңмқҳ мһ…л Ҙ мқјлҘ  (Eq. 3)
The Maxwell tension $T = B_\perp B / (4\pi)$ resists footpoint motion at speed $v$, so:
$$W \approx v T = \frac{v B_\perp B}{4\pi} = \frac{B^2}{4\pi}\,\frac{v^2 t}{L}$$

**Key scaling**: Power grows linearly in time until reconnection saturates the buildup.

### Saturation condition / нҸ¬нҷ” мЎ°кұҙ
Setting $W = W_\text{required} \sim 10^7$ erg cmвҒ»ВІ sвҒ»В№:
$$t_\text{sat} = \frac{4\pi W_\text{required} L}{B^2 v^2}$$

Worked example for active-region loop:
- $B = 100$ G вҮ’ $B^2/(4\pi) = 800$ erg/cmВі
- $v = 0.5$ km/s = $5 \times 10^4$ cm/s вҮ’ $v^2 = 2.5 \times 10^9$ cmВІ/sВІ
- $L = 10^{10}$ cm
- $W_\text{required} = 10^7$ erg/cmВІ/s
- $t_\text{sat} = (10^7 \times 10^{10}) / (800 \times 2.5 \times 10^9) = 10^{17} / (2 \times 10^{12}) = 5 \times 10^4$ s вң“

### Random walk parameters / л¬ҙмһ‘мң„ ліҙн–ү л§Өк°ңліҖмҲҳ
- Total path: $vt = 2.5 \times 10^4$ km (= supergranule diameter)
- Step length: $l = v\tau = 0.5 \text{ km/s} \times 500 \text{ s} = 250$ km
- Number of steps: $n = t/\tau = 5 \times 10^4 / 500 = 100$
- Wrap length along bundle: $\Delta L = L/n = 10^{10}/100 = 10^8$ cm = $10^3$ km

### Characteristic nanoflare energy / нҠ№м§•м Ғ лӮҳл…ён”Ңл Ҳм–ҙ м—җл„Ҳм§Җ
$$\boxed{\mathcal{E} = \frac{l^2 \Delta L\, B_\perp^2}{8\pi}}$$

Worked example:
- $l = 250$ km = $2.5 \times 10^7$ cm
- $\Delta L = 10^3$ km = $10^8$ cm
- $B_\perp = 25$ G вҮ’ $B_\perp^2 / (8\pi) = 25$ erg/cmВі
- Volume: $V = l^2 \Delta L = (2.5 \times 10^7)^2 \times 10^8 = 6.25 \times 10^{22}$ cmВі
- $\mathcal{E} = 25 \times 6.25 \times 10^{22} = 1.6 \times 10^{24}$ erg

(Parker rounds to $6 \times 10^{24}$ erg, close to order-of-magnitude.)

### Nanoflare rate / лӮҳл…ён”Ңл Ҳм–ҙ л°ңмғқлҘ 
For an active region of area $A$:
$$N = \frac{W \cdot A}{\mathcal{E}}$$

For $A = 10^{19}$ cmВІ (typical active region): $N = 10^7 \times 10^{19} / 10^{24} = 10^2$ sвҒ»В№ вҖ” hundreds per second per active region.

### Comparison: Sweet-Parker vs. Petschek vs. nanoflare / л№„көҗ
| Quantity / м–‘ | Active-region loop | X-ray bright point |
|---|---|---|
| Loop length $L$ | $10^{10}$ cm | $10^9$ cm |
| Saturation time $t$ | $5 \times 10^4$ s (14 hr) | $5 \times 10^3$ s (1.4 hr) |
| Random steps $n$ | 100 | 10 |
| Step length $l$ | 250 km | 250 km |
| Wrap length $\Delta L$ | $10^3$ km | $10^3$ km |
| Per-discontinuity energy $\mathcal{E}$ | $10^{24}$ erg | $10^{24}$ erg |
| Surface brightness | $10^7$ erg cmвҒ»ВІ sвҒ»В№ | $10^7$ erg cmвҒ»ВІ sвҒ»В№ |

**Scale-invariance** of brightness emerges because $\mathcal{E}$, $l$, $\Delta L$ are all independent of $L$ вҖ” only $t$ and $n$ scale.
