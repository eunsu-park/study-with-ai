# 78. Extreme Solar Events / 극한 태양 사건

---

## 1. Core Contribution / 핵심 기여

Cliver, Schrijver, Shibata, and Usoskin present a 143-page encyclopedic review of extreme solar and solar-terrestrial events, tracing the field from Carrington's 1 September 1859 flare observation through the 2012 discoveries of the *Kepler* superflare population and the Miyake et al. 774–775 AD cosmogenic radionuclide event. The review systematically tabulates directly observed, inferred/reconstructed, and theoretically-derived worst-case magnitudes for seven phenomena: sunspot groups, solar flares, flares on Sun-like stars, solar radio bursts, coronal mass ejections (CMEs), geomagnetic storms/aurorae, and solar energetic particle (SEP) events. For each phenomenon, occurrence frequency distributions (OFDs) fitted with modified exponential and power-law functions yield 100-year and 1000-year event estimates, culminating in a master synthesis Table 10.

본 리뷰는 극한 태양 사건 연구의 진화를 1859년 9월 1일 Carrington 플레어 관측부터 2012년 *Kepler* 슈퍼플레어 모집단 및 Miyake et al.의 774–775 AD 우주선 방사성핵종 사건 발견까지 추적하는 143쪽 백과사전적 개관이다. 흑점군, 태양 플레어, 태양형(Sun-like) 별 플레어, 태양 전파 버스트, 코로나 질량 방출(CME), 지자기 폭풍/오로라, 태양 양성자(SEP) 사건의 7개 현상에 대해 직접 관측값, 추론/재구성 값, 이론적 worst-case 한계값을 체계적으로 표로 정리한다. 각 현상에 대해 수정 지수 함수 및 멱법칙 함수로 적합된 발생 빈도 분포(OFD)로 100년 및 1000년 사건 크기를 추정하며, 이는 마스터 종합 Table 10으로 집약된다. 저자들은 또한 극한 사건이 단지 매우 큰 사건인지(Black Swan), 아니면 다른 물리가 작동하는 dragon king인지에 대한 철학적 질문을 제기하며, 거대 decimetric radio burst와 거대 지자기 폭풍(field-aligned/auroral current 영향)에서 dragon king 증거를 확인한다.

## 2. Reading Notes / 읽기 노트

### Part I: Introduction and Historical Arc (§1, pp. 3–8) / 서론과 역사적 호

**p. 3**: Richard Carrington (1859) and Richard Hodgson independently observed the 1 September 1859 flare in integrated white light — the first solar flare recorded. Carrington's drawing of sunspot group 520 (Fig. 1) shows the transient bright emission patches (A, B) and final positions (C, D). The associated geomagnetic storm of 2 September 1859 was so intense that the magnetometers at Greenwich and Kew were driven off-scale; systematic records only dated from the 1830s.

**p. 4–5**: The "surge in interest" was triggered by Tsurutani et al. (2003) who analyzed the off-scale manual observations from Colaba (Bombay) at ~10-min intervals. This exposed the Carrington storm's extreme strength. ADS citations to Carrington's 1859 *MNRAS* paper (Fig. 2) show a dramatic rise from near-zero in 1970 to ~40/year by 2020. The journal *Space Weather* launched in 2003.

**p. 5–7**: Two transformative 2012 discoveries:
- **Miyake et al. (2012)**: High-time-resolution ¹⁴C measurements in two Japanese cedar trees showed a transient ~12‰ increase in Δ¹⁴C from 774 to 775 AD (Fig. 3). Confirmed as solar SEP origin by a multi-isotope investigation (Mekhaldi et al. 2015).
- **Maehara et al. (2012)**: First 120 days of *Kepler* observations revealed 14 superflares (energy > 10³⁴ erg) on 14,000 Sun-like stars. They calculated that a bolometric energy > 10³⁴ erg flare could occur on a Sun-like star once every ~800 years.
- **23 July 2012 backside CME**: STEREO observed a major eruption that, had it occurred on the Earth-facing side, would have produced a storm greater than Carrington (Baker et al. 2013, Liu et al. 2014).

### Part II: Sunspot Groups (§2, pp. 8–11) / 흑점군

**p. 8**: Primary data base — Royal Greenwich Observatory (RGO) from 1874 to 1976, extended by Solar Observing Optical Network (SOON) and others. Area measured in millionths of a solar hemisphere (μSH; 1 μSH = 3.0 × 10⁶ km²).

**p. 9–10**: Four distribution functions have been tried: lognormal (Baumann & Solanki 2005), bi-lognormal (Nagovitsyn et al.), Weibull (Muñoz-Jaramillo et al. 2015), and modified exponential (Gopalswamy 2018). Fig. 6 shows the Gopalswamy cumulative distribution for 41,433 spot groups (May 1874 – December 2016) with:
- Modified exponential fit: a=2.5, b=3.3, c=0.8 → 100-yr spot area = **5800 μSH**, 1000-yr = **8200 μSH**
- Power-law fit: α=11.67, β=3.55 → 100-yr = 7100 μSH, 1000-yr = 13,600 μSH

The largest observed group since 1874 was Greenwich group 14886 on 8 April 1947 with a corrected area of **6132 μSH** (close to the exponential 100-yr estimate).

### Part III: Solar Flares (§3.1, pp. 12–30) / 태양 플레어

**p. 12 — GOES SXR classification**: Classes A1-9, B1-9, C1-9, M1-9, X1-9 correspond to peak 1-8 Å fluxes 1-9 × 10⁻ⁿ W m⁻² where n = 8, 7, 6, 5, 4 for A, B, C, M, X respectively. Flares with peak intensities ≥ 10⁻³ W m⁻² are referred to as X10 and above.

**p. 12**: The largest GOES SXR flare to date was on **4 November 2003**. The 1-8 Å detector saturated at X18.4, with estimated class **X35 ± 5** based on sudden ionospheric disturbance (SID) and magnetic crochet observations. The Carrington flare is estimated at **X45 ± 5** based on the magnetic crochet (a type of SID recorded by ground magnetometers). The inferred flare for the 774 AD SEP event is **X285 ± 140** (Sect. 7.8).

**p. 13 — Flare frequency distribution**: Power-law slope of -1.8 to -2.3 is characteristic (self-organized criticality; Lu & Hamilton 1991). Veronig et al. (2002a) found slope -2.11 ± 0.13 for ~50,000 GOES flares (1976–2000). Gopalswamy (2018) for 55,285 flares (1969–2016):
- Weibull: a=4.7, γ=-3.3, η=2.6, m=1.0
- Power-law: Y = -7.49 – 2.31X
→ 100-yr flare: **X44** (exponential) vs. **X42** (power-law); 1000-yr: **X101** vs. **X115**; 10,000-yr: **X200** (exp) vs. **X310** (PL).

**p. 15–16 — White-light flares**: The TIM (Total Irradiance Monitor) on SORCE detected the 4 November 2003 X17 flare bolometric energy at **4.3 × 10³²** erg (Woods et al. 2006). *Kepler* superflares reach bolometric energies **10³⁴–10³⁵ erg**, exceeding the largest solar flare by a factor of ~ 100. Detection completeness for 10³⁴ (10³³) erg stellar flares is ~ 0.1 (0.001).

**p. 17 — Neupert effect**: The integral of impulsive-phase nonthermal emission (hard X-ray, microwave) resembles the rise of the SXR light curve (Neupert 1968). Fig. 9 shows the inverse aspect — hard X-ray as derivative of SXR — for the 28 October 2013 flare. The Neupert effect provides a link between SXR as size measure and bolometric (mostly white-light) energy, allowing comparison of stellar white-light superflares to solar CMX classification.

**p. 21 — Flare energetics (Emslie et al. 2012)**: For 6 eruptive flares, average E_fm (free magnetic energy) ~ 15 × 10³² erg, with CME kinetic energy ~ 3× bolometric flare energy. Energy partition:
- Magnetic free energy (100%)
- CME kinetic + gravitational (~ 75%)
- Flare bolometric (~ 25%)
- SEPs, flare electrons, peak SXR plasma, etc. (smaller fractions)

**p. 24 — TSI to bolometric to SXR class (Schrijver et al. 2012; Kretzschmar 2011):**
$$\mathcal{F}_\text{TSI} = 2.4 \times 10^{12}\,\mathcal{F}_\text{GOES}^{0.65 \pm 0.05} \quad (\text{erg})$$
$$\mathcal{F}_\text{GOES} = 2.8 \times 10^{29}\left(\frac{\mathcal{C}_\text{GOES}}{\mathcal{C}_\text{GOES,X1}}\right)^{1.10} \quad (\text{erg})$$
$$\mathcal{F}_\text{TSI} = 0.33 \times 10^{32}\left(\frac{\mathcal{C}_\text{GOES}}{\mathcal{C}_\text{GOES,X1}}\right)^{0.72} \quad (\text{erg})$$
Thus 10³² (10³³) erg corresponds to ~ X5 (~ X115) flare.

**p. 24–30 — Largest possible flare based on April 1947 spot group**:
Three independent estimates for unsigned magnetic flux of 6 × 10²³ Mx (AR total):
1. Reconnection flux (Kazachenko et al. 2017): ~ X105, 9.4 × 10³² erg
2. **Reconnection flux (Tschernitz et al. 2018): X180 (-100, +300), 1.4 × 10³³ erg ← preferred**
3. Active region energy (Aulanier et al. 2013): ~ X240, 1.7 × 10³³ erg

The preferred X180 estimate (Table 1) encompasses the ~ X285 SXR class inferred for 774 AD.

### Part IV: Flares on Sun-like Stars (§3.2, pp. 31–46) / 태양형 별 플레어

**p. 31–32 — Superflare definition**: Stellar flares with radiative energy ≥ 10³³ erg (~ 2–3× greater than the largest directly observed solar flare; Emslie et al. 2012). Schaefer et al. (2000) identified 9 superflares ~ 10³³⁻³⁸ erg on G-type main sequence stars. Rubenstein & Schaefer (2000) argued these required close-in "hot Jupiters", implying Sun immune.

**p. 33 — Shibata's challenge**: Kazunari Shibata questioned the hot-Jupiter hypothesis (RS CVn rotation is tidal) and encouraged young researchers (Maehara, Shibayama, Notsu, Nagao, Kusaba) to search *Kepler* data. Result: **Maehara et al. (2012) Nature paper**: 365 superflares on 148 G-type main sequence stars, including 14 with energy > 10³⁴ erg. Restrictive criteria yielded 9 superflares on 5 "Sun-like" stars (Tₑff 5600–6000 K, P_rot 11–17 d).

**p. 33 — Shibayama et al. (2013) extension**: 500 days of *Kepler* data, 1547 superflares on 279 G-type stars, 44 on 19 Sun-like stars; 3 with rotation > 25 d (slower than Sun). Superflare frequency on Sun-like stars: one 10³⁴ erg event every ~500 years (Maehara et al. 2015), one 10³³ erg every ~2000–3000 years (Notsu et al.), one 10³³ erg every ~6000 years with gyrochronology correction (Okamoto et al. 2021).

**p. 35–39 — Verifying Sun-like nature**: High-Dispersion Spectrograph on Subaru (Notsu et al. 2015a) verified 7 Sun-like stars (later 8, then 15 with *Gaia*-DR2). Properties comparable to Sun (Table 2, 3): Tₑff 5600–6000 K, log g > 4.0, [Fe/H] near solar, rotation P_rot > 10–20 d.

**p. 42 — Shibata et al. (2013) energy-area scaling**:
$$E_\text{flare} \approx fE_\text{mag} \approx f\frac{B^2}{8\pi}A_\text{spot}^{3/2} \approx 7\times 10^{32}\,\text{erg}\left(\frac{f}{0.1}\right)\left(\frac{B}{10^3\,\text{G}}\right)^2\left(\frac{A_\text{spot}}{3\times 10^{19}\,\text{cm}^2}\right)^{3/2}$$

**Worked example**: For A_spot / (2πR²_⊙) = 0.001 (i.e., 0.1% of solar hemisphere = 10³ μSH), B = 3000 G, f = 0.1 — solve for E_flare:
- With B = 3000 G (vs. 1000 G normalization), B-squared factor is 9×.
- A_spot ≈ 3×10¹⁹ cm² (0.001 × 2πR²_⊙ ≈ 3×10¹⁹ cm²).
- E_flare ≈ 7×10³² × (0.1/0.1) × (3000/1000)² × 1.0 = 6.3 × 10³³ erg.
This explains why stars with A_spot ~ 10³ μSH and stronger fields (~3 kG) produce 10³³-10³⁴ erg superflares.

**p. 45–46 — Unified frequency distribution**: Fig. 24 shows a single power-law dN/dE ~ E^(-1.8) extending from nanoflares through microflares, solar flares, to superflares across ~14 orders of magnitude in energy, suggesting the same self-organized criticality / reconnection physics operates across this range.

### Part V: Extreme Radio Bursts (§4, pp. 47–58) / 극한 전파 버스트

**p. 48**: Radio frequency interference from a 6 December 2006 decimetric burst (300-3000 MHz) severely degraded GPS performance, signaling a modern hazard. Giersch et al. (2017) analyzed 1.415 GHz RSTN 1996–2010 data (Fig. 25): power-law slope -1.815 ± 0.01. 100-yr event = **3.2 × 10⁶ sfu**; 1000-yr = **6.1 × 10⁷ sfu** (1 sfu = 10⁻²² W m⁻² Hz⁻¹).

**p. 49–55**: Table 4 lists 14 events with 1.0-1.6 GHz peak fluxes ≥ 80,000 sfu from 1966–2015. The 6 December 2006 event had ~10⁶ sfu at 1.4 GHz (Owens Valley FAST). Fig. 30 shows the 1 GHz cumulative density function exhibits anomalous behavior vs. other frequencies — supporting dragon-king interpretation via electron cyclotron maser (ECM) emission for the largest bursts. The ECM mechanism requires f_ce >> f_pe in post-eruption density cavities (Fig. 33).

### Part VI: Coronal Mass Ejections (§5, pp. 60–65) / 코로나 질량 방출

**p. 60 — CME speed organizes effects** (Fig. 34, Gopalswamy 2018): average speeds for populations — all (450), metric type II (611), magnetic clouds (782), ICMEs w/o flux rope (955), in-situ shocks (966), non-recurrent geomagnetic storms (1007), halo (1089), decametric type II (1194), GLE proton events (1557), SEP events (~1557), with fastest ≥ 2000 km s⁻¹.

**p. 61–63 — CME OFD** (Gopalswamy 2018; 27,000 CMEs 1996–March 2016):
- Modified exponential: 100-yr **3800 km/s**, 1000-yr **4700 km/s**
- Power-law: 100-yr **4500 km/s**, 1000-yr **6600 km/s**
- Fastest LASCO CME: 3387 km s⁻¹ (10 November 2004, mass 9.5 × 10¹⁵ g)
- Most energetic: **4.2 × 10³³ erg** on 9 September 2005
- Theoretical maximum (April 1947 AR, B = 6.1 kG): ~ 6700 km s⁻¹, total energy ~ 4 × 10³⁶ erg

**p. 63–65 — Fast transit ICMEs** (Table 5, 26 events with transit time ≤ 30 h): Record is **14.6 h** on 4 August 1972. The 23 July 2012 backside CME had 18.6 h transit (Russell, Baker, Liu 2013, 2014). 1859 transit ≤ 17.1 h (Hayakawa et al. 2022).

### Part VII: Geomagnetic Storms and Aurorae (§6, pp. 65–76) / 지자기 폭풍과 오로라

**p. 65 — Dst definition**: Hourly-average geomagnetic index; minimum Dst during a storm = storm intensity. Severe: -600 nT < Dst < -250 nT; Extreme: Dst < -600 nT (Riley & Love 2017).

**p. 66 — Waiting times for Dst ≤ -600 nT storms** (Table 6): median 55 years.
- Median -850 nT storm waiting time: 93 years.
- 100-yr Dst estimates: range from -542 nT (Love 2020, upper-limit lognormal) to **-603 nT** (Gopalswamy 2018, modified exponential) to -774 nT (power-law).
- 1000-yr estimates: **-845 nT** (exponential), -1470 nT (power-law), -1100 nT (extreme value Love et al. 2015).

**p. 66–67 — Extreme storm constraints**:
- 4 August 1972 ICME: if B had been southward, Dst ~ **-1400 nT** (Gonzalez et al. 2011).
- 22–23 July 2012 ICME (if Earth-directed): Dst ~ **-1182 nT** (Baker et al. 2013).
- Vasyliūnas (2011) upper limit from magnetosphere plasma stress balance: Dst ~ -2500 nT.

**p. 68–75 — Reconstructing pre-modern storms from low-latitude aurora** (Table 7):
- **1 September 1770**: aurora down to ILAT ~ 32.5° (Hayakawa et al. 2017); estimated Dst ~ -780 nT. Contemporary Japanese aurora drawing in Fig. 37.
- **August/September 1859 (Carrington)**: Dst = -949 ± 30 nT (Hayakawa et al. 2022 from Colaba). Aurora reached N23.2° geomagnetic latitude (Hayakawa et al. 2018). Colaba H-component off-scale negative swing of ~1600 nT in Fig. 40.
- **4 February 1872**: aurora in Bombay (deflector magnet inserted at Colaba), ILAT ≤ 41.8°. Estimated Dst ~ -1000 to -1250 nT.
- **15 May 1921**: Dst ~ -907 ± 132 nT (Love et al. 2019c). Aurora at Apia, Samoa (ILAT 16.2°S) at peak height 22 km (Fig. 42 positive bay coincident with overhead aurora).

**p. 71 — Relationship between lowest ILAT of overhead aurora and minimum Dst** (Yokoyama et al. 1998, empirical):
$$\text{Dst(min)} = -2200 \cos(\text{ILAT})^6 + 12 \quad [\text{nT}]$$

**p. 74–76 — Global substorms**: The simultaneity of low-latitude magnetogram features (Colaba 1859, Rome, Apia 1921) with overhead auroral activity suggests **field-aligned/ionospheric currents** contribute to Dst in extreme storms. This "global substorm" aspect makes extreme storms pre-dominant space weather threat as they can produce large dB/dt at geomagnetic latitudes far equatorward of normal auroral zones.

### Part VIII: Solar Energetic Proton (SEP) Events (§7, pp. 77–103) / 태양 양성자 사건

**p. 77–83 — Acceleration at the Sun**: SEPs primarily accelerated by CME-driven shocks (Reames 2013). Flare-resident acceleration also contributes as evidenced by > 300 MeV γ-ray emission from pion decay during impulsive phases (Ackermann et al. 2012, 2017). The "flare vs. shock" debate is far from over.

**p. 83–85 — OFD for F₃₀** (Fig. 47, Gopalswamy 2018 from 216 events 1987–2016):
- Weibull: a=0.9, γ=8.2, η=1.7, m=1.0
- Power-law: Y = 9.67 – 1.13X
- 100-yr F₃₀ = **6.6 × 10⁹ cm⁻²** (exponential), **2.1 × 10¹⁰** (power-law)
- 1000-yr F₃₀ = **10¹⁰** (exp), **1.6 × 10¹¹** (PL)
- Largest single observed (14 July 2000): 4.3 × 10⁹ cm⁻²
- Largest composite (4-7 August 1972): 8.4 × 10⁹ cm⁻²

**p. 85–87 — Nitrate proxy discredited**: McCracken et al. (2001) used ice-core nitrate to infer 70 strong SEP events 1561–1950 with F₃₀ up to 2 × 10¹⁰ cm⁻² for 1859. **Wolff et al. (2012)** showed the 1859 nitrate peak is a biomass burning plume (co-located ammonium, formate, black carbon, vanillic acid). Ultimate test: 774 AD and 993 AD cosmogenic events show **no corresponding nitrate peaks** (Sukhodolov et al. 2017). The nitrate proxy is dismissed.

**p. 87–88 — Cosmogenic radionuclides** (Paper #52 connection):
- **¹⁴C**: half-life 5.73 × 10³ yr; produced via ¹⁴N(n,p)¹⁴C; measured as Δ¹⁴C in tree rings with 1-, 5-, 10-year resolution (IntCal dataset). Holocene coverage ~ 12 kyr.
- **¹⁰Be**: half-life 1.36 × 10⁶ yr; produced by spallation of O, N; attached to aerosols → ice cores (Greenland, Antarctica). Can date back million years.
- **³⁶Cl**: half-life 3.01 × 10⁵ yr; spallation of atmospheric ⁴⁰Ar. Sensitive to lower-energy (~30 MeV) primaries; diagnostic of spectrum hardness.
- Spectrum hardness ratio **³⁶Cl/¹⁰Be ~ 1.2 (hard)** to 6 (soft); 774 AD and 993 AD are hard-spectrum events.

**p. 88–92 — 774 AD event (largest known SEP event)**:
Miyake et al. (2012) high-resolution Δ¹⁴C in two Japanese cedars showed ~12‰ increase 774–775 AD (Fig. 3, 48). Fast rise (1–2 yr) / gradual decline (decades, carbon cycle). Confirmed by trees worldwide (Fig. 48: Poland, Yamal, Germany, Altai, Japan, California, New Zealand), and by ice-core ¹⁰Be and ³⁶Cl.

Global ¹⁴C production estimates Q_774 (Table 9):
| Model | Q_774 (× 10⁸ cm⁻²) |
|---|---|
| 6-Box (Usoskin 2013) | 1.1–1.5 |
| 6-Box (Pavlov 2013) | 1.7 |
| 11-Box (Güttler 2015) | 2.2 |
| Box-diffusion (Mekhaldi 2015) | 2.16 |
| 11-Box (Uusitalo 2018) | 2.18 |
| **22-Box (Büntgen 2018)** | **1.88 ± 0.1** (current standard) |

**p. 92 — 774 AD proton spectrum** (Mekhaldi et al. 2015, from ¹⁴C + ¹⁰Be + ³⁶Cl):
Spectrum hardness S_I = log(F₃₀/F₂₀₀) ~ 0.8 (hard, comparable to GLE #5 23 February 1956 with S_I = 1.0). 774 AD F₂₀₀ ≈ 10¹⁰ cm⁻², which is **~ 70 × the 1956 GLE** F₂₀₀.

**p. 93 — 993 AD and earlier events**:
Miyake et al. (2013, 2020a) found similar event at 993–994 AD. Additional confirmed events: 7176 BC (Paleari et al. 2022, Brehm et al. 2022), 5259 BC (Brehm et al. 2022), ca. 660 BC (O'Hare et al. 2019, Park et al. 2017, Sakurai et al. 2020). Three candidate events (5410 BC, 1052 AD, 1279 AD, Miyake 2021, Brehm 2021) not yet independently confirmed. Possibly 3372 BC dismissed by Jull et al. (2021).

**p. 96–99 — Composite F₃₀ OFD** (Fig. 52):
Combines space-era GOES data (1954–2020, triangles), confirmed cosmogenic events (stars), and unconfirmed candidates (open circles). Weibull fit: P(>F₃₀) = exp(-(F₃₀ / 3.66 × 10⁸)^0.4).
- 100-yr F₃₀ ~ **10¹⁰** cm⁻²
- 1000-yr F₃₀ ~ **5 × 10¹⁰** cm⁻²
- 10,000-yr F₃₀ ~ **10¹¹** cm⁻²
- No F₃₀ > 10¹¹ cm⁻² events over 11 millennia (Usoskin 2017).

**p. 101–103 — Does 774 AD imply an X3000 flare? (§7.8)**:
Early estimates of 774 AD bolometric energy ranged from 10³⁴–10³⁶ erg (Miyake 2012; Shibata 2013; Maehara 2015), implying X3000 via Eq. (5). Cliver et al. (2020b) used F₂₀₀ vs. SXR correlation for hard-spectrum GLEs (Fig. 56):

$$\log(F_{200}) = 1.5611 \log(\text{SXR}_\text{int}) + 12.48 \quad (\text{1956 GLE reference line})$$
$$\log(F_{200}) = 1.5611 \log(\text{SXR}_\text{int}) + 12.09 \quad (\text{X30 reference line})$$

Using F₂₀₀ = 8.8 × 10⁹ cm⁻² and Q_774/Q_1956 = 62, Cliver et al. (2020b) obtained **SXR class = X285 ± 140** and bolometric = **1.9 ± 0.7 × 10³³ erg**. This is the superflare threshold (10³³ erg) and ~4–5× Carrington. If 774 AD was 3 closely-spaced events (a la August 1972): reduces to ~ X140 ± 70 (1.2 ± 0.5 × 10³³ erg) per flare. Kong et al. (2017) showed a streamer at the W55 solar footpoint can enhance > 100 MeV proton acceleration by ~ 2 orders of magnitude — Cliver speculated this "favorable magnetic topology" may explain 774 AD.

**p. 108 — Important note on GOES SXR recalibration**: Pre-GOES-16 (2017) SXR peak fluxes need to be increased by factor 1/0.7 = 1.43. So X45 ± 5 → X64.4 ± 7.2 for Carrington; X285 ± 140 → X410 ± 200 for 774 AD (if recalibrated).

**p. 109–111 — Is the Sun a Sun-like star?**: The principal concerns:
1. *Kepler* Sun-like superflare stars have strongly spot-dominated variability, whereas the Sun is facula-dominated (Willson & Hudson 1988).
2. No spectroscopically confirmed Sun-like superflare stars with P_rot > 25 d.
3. Reinhold et al. (2020): 369 solar-like stars with P_rot 20–30 d have variability ~ 5× the Sun's — hints the Sun is in an "uncharacteristically subdued" state.

**p. 111–112 — Black Swans and Dragon Kings**:
- **Black Swan** (Taleb 2007): unpredictable, massive impact, retrospectively rationalizable. Extreme space weather events are NOT black swans: their signatures are observed, and we can prepare.
- **Dragon King** (Sornette 2009): extreme events not belonging to the same population, arising from amplifying mechanisms. Evidence for dragon kings in:
  1. Intense decimetric radio bursts (ECM emission — different physics)
  2. Largest geomagnetic storms (field-aligned/auroral currents affecting low-latitude magnetograms)
  3. Possibly 774 AD SEP event (streamer-enhanced proton acceleration; gap in F₂₀₀ OFD)

## 3. Key Takeaways / 핵심 시사점

1. **The Carrington event is no longer the worst-case scenario.** — Carrington는 더 이상 worst-case가 아니다. The inferred 774 AD SEP event has F₂₀₀ ~70× the 1956 GLE and an associated SXR flare of X285 ± 140 (1.9 ± 0.7 × 10³³ erg), qualifying as a superflare by the 10³³ erg threshold. Carrington (X45 ± 5, ~5 × 10³² erg) is merely the largest *observed* event. / 774 AD 사건은 F₂₀₀이 1956 GLE의 ~70배이며, 관련 SXR 플레어는 X285 ± 140 (1.9 ± 0.7 × 10³³ erg)로 10³³ erg 슈퍼플레어 기준을 만족한다.

2. **The Sun probably can produce ~ 10³³ erg superflares, perhaps once per millennium.** — Sun은 천 년에 한 번 정도 10³³ erg 슈퍼플레어 가능. The 10,000-year flare estimate of ~ 2 × 10³³ erg (X200–X310) from GOES OFD roughly matches the 774 AD reconstruction and the Kepler superflare occurrence rate on Sun-like stars (~ 1 per 2000–6000 years for 10³³ erg). Shibata et al. (2013) A^(3/2) scaling shows stars with 3× larger spot areas (≥ 3000 μSH) or 3× stronger fields (9 kG) can reach 10³⁴–10³⁵ erg. / 2 × 10³³ erg의 10,000년 플레어 추정치는 774 AD 재구성과 Kepler 슈퍼플레어 빈도와 일치.

3. **Miyake events are real and driven by SEP events, not supernovae or GRBs.** — Miyake 사건은 초신성이나 GRB가 아닌 SEP 사건에 의해 구동된다. The latitudinal symmetry of ¹⁴C signals, the presence of ¹⁰Be signatures (Mekhaldi et al. 2015), and identification of at least five confirmed events (7176 BC, 5259 BC, 660 BC, 774 AD, 993 AD) establish the SEP paradigm. / ¹⁴C의 위도 대칭성과 ¹⁰Be 신호, 5개 확인 사건의 존재는 SEP 패러다임 확립.

4. **The nitrate ice-core proxy for SEP events is discredited.** — 질산염 얼음 코어 SEP 프록시는 폐기됨. Wolff et al. (2012) showed the 1859 nitrate peak is biomass-burning contamination (co-located ammonium, black carbon, vanillic acid). No nitrate response to 774/993 AD events. Cosmogenic radionuclides (¹⁴C, ¹⁰Be, ³⁶Cl) in trees and ice are the only reliable proxies. / 1859년 질산염 peak는 바이오매스 연소 오염으로 판명. ¹⁴C, ¹⁰Be, ³⁶Cl만이 신뢰 가능.

5. **Extreme geomagnetic storms are "global substorms" — low-latitude magnetograms are affected by high-latitude auroral currents.** — 극한 지자기 폭풍은 global substorm. The coincidence in time of off-scale excursions at low-latitude stations (Colaba 1859, Apia 1921) with overhead auroral activity demonstrates field-aligned/auroral currents contribute to Dst. This extends the dB/dt threat to mid-latitudes, making these storms the pre-dominant space weather hazard. / Colaba 1859 및 Apia 1921의 저위도 magnetogram 이상과 오로라 활동의 시간 일치는 field-aligned current의 영향을 증명.

6. **100-year and 1000-year event estimates carry real uncertainty from fit-function choice.** — fit 함수 선택에 의한 실제 불확실성. Modified exponential vs. power-law differ by factor 1.5–5× at 100-yr and 2–10× at 1000-yr (e.g., CME kinetic energy: 100 vs. 300 × 10³² erg at 1000-yr). Three storms (1859, 1872, 1921) surpassed the exponential-based 1000-yr value of -845 nT within ~ 160 years — power-law may be more appropriate for the storm tail. / 세 폭풍(1859, 1872, 1921)이 ~160년 내 exponential 기반 1000-yr 추정치 -845 nT를 넘었음 — 꼬리는 power-law가 더 적절할 수 있음.

7. **Dragon kings exist in solar activity — different physics governs the largest events.** — 가장 큰 사건에는 다른 물리가 작동. Great (≥ 10⁵ sfu) decimetric radio bursts are dragon kings driven by electron cyclotron maser emission (needs f_ce >> f_pe, density cavity). The gap in the F₂₀₀ OFD between the 1956 GLE and the 774 AD event (factor of 70) suggests 774 AD may involve streamer-enhanced proton acceleration (Kong et al. 2017). Extreme storms also show dragon-king character via field-aligned currents. / 거대 decimetric burst는 electron cyclotron maser에 의한 dragon king; F₂₀₀ OFD의 gap은 streamer enhancement 가능성 시사.

8. **The Sun may not be a "Sun-like" star.** — Sun은 "Sun-like" 별이 아닐 수 있다. *Kepler* Sun-like superflare stars are spot-dominated; the Sun is facula-dominated. No spectroscopically confirmed Sun-like superflare star has P_rot > 25 days. Reinhold et al. (2020) found the Sun's variability ~ 5× lower than 369 solar-like peers — possibly a subdued phase. This makes superflare probability extrapolation from stellar data uncertain. / Kepler Sun-like 슈퍼플레어 별은 spot-dominated, Sun은 facula-dominated; 스펙트럼 확인된 P_rot > 25일 슈퍼플레어 별 없음.

## 4. Mathematical Summary / 수학적 요약

### 4.1 Occurrence Frequency Distributions (OFDs) / 발생 빈도 분포

**Modified exponential (Gopalswamy 2018):**
$$Y = a\left[1 - \exp\left(-\frac{-X + b}{c}\right)\right]$$
where $X = \log_{10}(\text{event size})$, $Y = \log_{10}(\text{annual rate})$.
- **a**: normalization; **b**: location; **c**: scale parameter (distribution spread in X).
- Fit via MPFIT (IDL) with initial guesses.
- **Use**: provides a realistic roll-off of the tail (exponential-like), often fits observed data over the full range.

**Power-law tail (Newman 2005; Clauset et al. 2009):**
$$Y = \alpha - \beta X$$
- MLE estimator used with lower bound X_min; **β**: slope (~1.8–2.3 for most solar phenomena).
- **Use**: good for the large-event tail; yields higher 1000-yr estimates than exponential.

**Weibull distribution (lifetime/extreme):**
$$P(>F) = \exp\left[-\left(\frac{F}{F_0}\right)^k\right]$$
- F_0: scale; k: shape parameter.
- Used for F₃₀ OFD (k = 0.4, F_0 = 3.66 × 10⁸ cm⁻²): flexible form interpolating exponential (k=1) and heavier tails.

**100-year / 1000-year event**: intersection of the fit with Y = -2 (1/100 per year) and Y = -3 (1/1000 per year).

### 4.2 Flare Energy Scaling / 플레어 에너지 스케일링

**Shibata et al. (2013) spot-area scaling:**
$$E_\text{flare} \approx f E_\text{mag} \approx f \frac{B^2}{8\pi} A_\text{spot}^{3/2}$$
- f: fraction of magnetic energy released (~ 0.1).
- B: photospheric field strength in the spot.
- A_spot: spot area; L = A^(1/2) scale length; Volume ~ L³ = A^(3/2).
- **Physical basis**: energy stored in B² / 8π over a volume of order A^(3/2) near the spot.

**Schrijver–Tschernitz–Kretzschmar chain (Eqs. 3, 4, 5):**
$$\mathcal{F}_\text{TSI} = 2.4 \times 10^{12}\,\mathcal{F}_\text{GOES}^{0.65 \pm 0.05} \quad \text{(erg)}$$
$$\mathcal{F}_\text{GOES} = 2.8 \times 10^{29}\left(\frac{\mathcal{C}_\text{GOES}}{\mathcal{C}_\text{GOES,X1}}\right)^{1.10} \quad \text{(erg)}$$
$$\mathcal{F}_\text{TSI} = 0.33 \times 10^{32}\left(\frac{\mathcal{C}_\text{GOES}}{\mathcal{C}_\text{GOES,X1}}\right)^{0.72} \quad \text{(erg)}$$
- Converts GOES 1–8 Å peak flux (W m⁻²) normalized to X1 → SXR fluence → total radiated (bolometric) energy.
- **Example**: X180 flare → TSI fluence = 0.33 × 10³² × 180^0.72 ≈ 1.4 × 10³³ erg.

### 4.3 774 AD → SXR class / 774 AD → SXR 등급

**Cliver et al. (2020b) F₂₀₀–SXR correlation for hard-spectrum GLEs:**
$$\log(F_{200}) = 1.5611 \log(\text{SXR}_\text{int}) + 12.48 \quad \text{(1956 GLE tie-point, Eq. 14)}$$
$$\log(F_{200}) = 1.5611 \log(\text{SXR}_\text{int}) + 12.09 \quad \text{(X30 tie-point, Eq. 15)}$$

**Worked derivation for 774 AD**:
- Q_774 / Q_1956 = 62 (Büntgen 22-box with geomagnetic correction ≈ 70).
- F₂₀₀(1956 GLE) = 1.4 × 10⁸ cm⁻² → F₂₀₀(774 AD) = 62 × 1.4 × 10⁸ ≈ 8.8 × 10⁹ cm⁻².
- From Eq. (14) for 1956-tie: $\log(8.8 \times 10^9) = 9.94 = 1.5611 \log(\text{SXR}) + 12.48$ → $\log(\text{SXR}) = (9.94 - 12.48)/1.5611 = -1.627$ → SXR = 10^(−1.627) = 2.36 × 10⁻² W m⁻² = **X236**.
- Averaged over both bridging lines with uncertainty: **X285 ± 140**.
- Bolometric via Eq. (5): $\mathcal{F}_\text{TSI} = 0.33 \times 10^{32} \times 285^{0.72} \approx 1.9 \times 10^{33}$ erg.

### 4.4 Geomagnetic storm Dst ↔ aurora ILAT / 지자기 폭풍 Dst ↔ 오로라 ILAT

**Yokoyama et al. (1998, empirical from 423 storms 1983–1991):**
$$\text{Dst(min)} = -2200 \cos^6(\text{ILAT}) + 12 \quad [\text{nT}]$$
- ILAT: invariant magnetic latitude of the lowest overhead aurora.
- **Example**: ILAT = 25° (1859 Colaba-inferred latitude): $\cos^6(25°) = 0.906^6 = 0.554$ → Dst ~ -2200 × 0.554 + 12 ≈ **-1207 nT** (within uncertainty range of historical estimates; Hayakawa 2022 Colaba direct = -949 nT).

### 4.5 Generalized Extreme Value (GEV) / 일반화 극값 분포

Block maxima $M_n = \max(X_1, \dots, X_n)$ asymptotically follow:
$$G_\xi(z) = \exp\left\{-\left[1 + \xi\left(\frac{z - \mu}{\sigma}\right)\right]^{-1/\xi}\right\}$$
- μ: location; σ: scale; ξ: shape.
- ξ > 0 → Fréchet (heavy tail), ξ = 0 → Gumbel (light tail), ξ < 0 → Weibull (bounded).
- **Return period** T = 1/(1 - G(z)) for block size = 1 year.
- Used by Thomson et al. (2011) for storm dB/dt: 100-yr 1000–4000 nT/min at 55–60° geomagnetic latitude.
